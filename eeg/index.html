Overview

When you imagine moving your left hand — without actually moving it — your brain generates measurable electrical patterns that are detectably different from imagining moving your right hand. This sounds almost like science fiction, but it's the basis of real brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) used today to help people with paralysis communicate or control prosthetics.

This project builds a complete pipeline to do exactly that: load raw brainwave recordings, clean them, extract meaningful features, and train classifiers that can tell left-hand from right-hand motor imagery with above-chance accuracy — all from a cap of 64 electrodes placed on the scalp.

The key phenomenon: Event-Related Desynchronisation (ERD). When you imagine moving a limb, the brain's motor cortex on the opposite side (contralateral) suppresses its alpha (8–13 Hz) and beta (13–30 Hz) rhythms. This dip in oscillatory power is the signal we're hunting.

2Extract frequency-band features (alpha, beta power at each electrode) using Welch's PSD.
3Compare three classifiers: Flat Band Power, CSP+LDA, and Riemannian MDM — measuring accuracy and AUC.

The Signal

EEG (electroencephalography) measures the tiny electrical voltages produced by millions of neurons firing in synchrony beneath the scalp. We place 64 electrodes in a standard "10-20" grid — named because electrodes sit at 10% or 20% intervals along the head. Each electrode captures a weighted sum of activity from the underlying cortex.

The signal is measured in microvolts (µV) — about a millionth of a volt, or roughly one-thousandth the voltage of a typical AA battery. It is buried in noise: muscle movement (EMG), eye blinks, heartbeats, and 50/60 Hz power-line interference all contaminate the recording. The preprocessing steps exist to scrub all of this away.

Figure 1 — Raw vs ICA-cleaned EEG signal. The raw signal (grey) contains large slow deflections from eye blinks, most visible in frontal channels Fp1/Fp2. Toggle to ICA Cleaned to see these removed while the motor-cortex channels (C3, C4) remain virtually unchanged.

Why do frontal channels show the biggest artifact? Eye muscles sit directly beneath Fp1 and Fp2. When you blink, a large dipole sweeps across the frontal electrodes. ICA decomposes the signal into 20 independent components and discards those that correlate strongly with the frontal channels — leaving the rest intact.

The data comes from the PhysioNet EEGBCI dataset — a publicly available benchmark containing 64-channel EEG from 109 subjects performing motor-imagery tasks. We use subjects 1–10 and runs 6, 10, and 14 (motor imagery only, no actual movement). Each run contains trials where participants were shown an arrow and asked to imagine moving the corresponding hand.

Preprocessing

Raw EEG is essentially unusable for classification. Before we can extract any meaningful signal, five processing steps clean the data and put all subjects on a common footing. 

# Step 1: Bandpass filter 1–40 Hz
# Removes DC drift (below 1 Hz)
# and EMG/noise (above 40 Hz)
raw.filter(
    1.0, 40.0,
    fir_window='hamming',
    verbose=False
)

Bandpass filter 1–40 Hz

Think of this like an audio equaliser that cuts the very low rumble (slow drift from sweat/electrode movement) and the very high hiss (muscle noise, power-line interference above 40 Hz).

We use a Hamming-windowed FIR filter — a standard choice that minimises ringing artefacts at band edges. This preserves the delta through gamma brain rhythms we care about.

# Step 2: Average re-reference
# Each channel = channel − mean(all channels)
raw.set_eeg_reference(
    'average',
    projection=True
)
raw.apply_proj()

Average re-reference

EEG voltages are always measured relative to some reference electrode. If that reference sits over an active area, it contaminates every channel.

Subtracting the mean across all 64 electrodes at each time point makes the measurement reference-independent, giving each channel a fair starting point.

# Step 3: ICA artifact removal
ica = mne.preprocessing.ICA(
    n_components=20,
    method='fastica',
    random_state=42
)
ica.fit(raw)

# Find eye-blink components via
# correlation with Fp1/Fp2
inds, _ = ica.find_bads_eog(
    raw, ch_name=['Fp1','Fp2']
)
ica.exclude = inds[:2]
ica.apply(cleaned)

ICA artifact removal

Independent Component Analysis mathematically unmixes the 64 EEG channels into 20 "source" components — each ideally reflecting one underlying source (a brain region, eye muscle, or heartbeat).

Components that correlate with the frontal "EOG surrogate" channels Fp1/Fp2 are labelled as eye-blink artefacts and zeroed out. The remaining 18+ components are then remixed back into 64 clean channels.

# Step 4: Extract epochs
# T1 = left hand, T2 = right hand
epochs = mne.Epochs(
    raw, events,
    event_id={'left':2,'right':3},
    tmin=-1.0,   # 1s before cue
    tmax=+4.0,   # 4s after cue
    baseline=(-1.0, 0.0)
)

Epoching (−1 to +4 s)

Rather than analysing a continuous hour-long stream, we cut out 5-second epochs around each experimental cue. The −1 s pre-cue period serves as a baseline: we subtract its mean to correct for any residual slow drift.

Event code T1 marks left-hand cues; T2 marks right-hand cues. Each subject contributes roughly 45 trials per condition.

Feature Engineering

Raw epoch data is a 3-D array: (n_epochs × 64 channels × 500 time samples). No classifier works well on 32,000 raw numbers per trial. We need to compress each epoch into a compact, informative feature vector.

Two complementary approaches are used, targeting different properties of the signal.

# Approach 1: Band Power (Welch PSD)
# For each epoch × channel:
psds, freqs = psd_array_welch(
    data,
    sfreq=160,
    fmin=8.0, fmax=13.0,  # alpha
    n_fft=int(160 * 2)   # 2s window
)
# Average power across frequencies
alpha_power = psds.mean(axis=-1)
# Shape: (n_epochs, n_channels)

Approach 1 — Band Power Features

For each epoch, we run Welch's PSD (power spectral density) on every channel: split the epoch into overlapping 2-second windows, compute the FFT of each, and average the squared magnitudes.

The result is a single number per channel per frequency band — the mean oscillatory power in that band. We use alpha and beta, giving 64 × 2 = 128 features per epoch.

Simple, interpretable, but spatially naïve — it treats all 64 channels equally rather than finding the optimal spatial combination.

# Approach 2: Common Spatial Patterns
# Learns spatial filters that maximally
# separate left vs right covariances
csp = CSP(
    n_components=6,
    log=True,          # log-variance
    reg='ledoit_wolf'  # regularisation
)
# Fit only on training data!
X_feat = csp.fit_transform(X_train, y_train)
# Shape: (n_epochs, 6) — compact!

Approach 2 — Common Spatial Patterns (CSP)

CSP is the gold standard for motor imagery EEG. It finds 6 spatial filters (weighted combinations of the 64 electrodes) that maximise the ratio of left-trial variance to right-trial variance — and vice versa.

The log-variance of each filtered signal becomes one feature. Just 6 numbers per epoch, yet far more discriminative than 128 raw band-power values because the filters are optimally tuned to the left/right difference.

Key constraint: CSP must be fitted only on training data inside each cross-validation fold to prevent data leakage.

When you imagine moving your left hand, the suppression flips to the right motor cortex (C4). This spatial asymmetry is the key signal that distinguishes the two classes.

Classification

With features in hand, we train three different classifiers and evaluate them with stratified 5-fold cross-validation done within each subject. The within-subject design matters: CSP spatial filters are highly subject-specific. Mixing subjects would dilute the filters and make results look artificially bad. 

# Stratified 5-fold, within-subject
skf = StratifiedKFold(
    n_splits=5,
    shuffle=True,
    random_state=42
)
for train, test in skf.split(X_s, y_s):
    csp = CSP().fit(X_s[train], y_s[train])
    X_feat = csp.transform(X_s[test])
    acc = clf.score(X_feat, y_s[test])

Why within-subject cross-validation?

CSP learns subject-specific spatial filters. If we mix subjects before fitting, the filters get averaged across anatomical differences and stop working well for any individual.

Instead: for each of 10 subjects, run 5 folds independently. Report mean accuracy per subject, then average across subjects. 10 × 5 = 50 model fits in total.

CSP is always fitted only on the training fold — never touching test data — to prevent data leakage from optimistic filtering.

The Hard Problem: Inter-Subject Variability

The most striking finding is not the mean accuracy — it's the variance. Some subjects are decoded with near-perfect accuracy; others perform no better than a coin flip. This is the central challenge of EEG-based BCIs and an open research problem.

Subjects 6 and 9 are near chance level. This is not a failure of the classifier — it reflects genuine differences in how individuals modulate their alpha/beta rhythms during motor imagery. Some people produce strong, spatially focal ERD; others produce diffuse, weak signals that are essentially indistinguishable from noise at the scalp level.

Why does this variability exist? Several factors contribute:

→Anatomical differences: skull thickness and conductivity vary, attenuating signals differently between individuals.
→Mental strategy: some subjects use vivid kinaesthetic imagery; others use purely visual mental imagery — producing different neural patterns.
→Fatigue and habituation: the effect of imagining the same movement 90 times can diminish the response in some subjects.
→Neural noise floor: individuals differ in how much spontaneous alpha power they generate at rest, making the ERD harder or easier to detect.
Implication for real-world BCIs: A system that works brilliantly for subject 1 (89% accuracy) is essentially useless for subject 6 (47%). This motivates personalised calibration protocols, adaptive classifiers, and neurofeedback training that teaches users to produce stronger ERD signals.
