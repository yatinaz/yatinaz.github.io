Chapter 01 · Digital Mental Health

TreadWill: A Computerised CBT Approach to Treat Mood Disorders

Working in Prof. Nitin Gupta's lab, the project started from a premise in cognitive behavioural therapy: people prone to depression default to negative interpretations of ambiguous situations, and that pattern can be trained out. TreadWill is a web portal that does this through mental imagery games, placing users in scenarios and asking them to generate positive responses. My role was the front-end engineering: making the portal work consistently across Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and mobile WebKit. The work was published in JMIR Games in 2023.

Chapter 02 · Computational Psychiatry

How Do 105 People Learn to Make Better Decisions?

The question behind this project was simpler to ask than to answer: how do people learn under uncertainty, and why do some learn differently? I fit hierarchical Bayesian RL models to 10,000+ decisions from 105 participants in a modified restless bandit task, where reward probabilities shifted unpredictably between trials. Using hBayesDM and NLME in R, the goal was to recover individual learning parameters: update rates, exploration tendencies, sensitivity to uncertainty. Learning rate variability turned out to be the strongest predictor of between-subject differences in reward sensitivity, with direct implications for understanding differential vulnerability to mood disorders. Presented at IIT Delhi Research Conclave 2023 and CogSci 2022.

2023 University of Western Australia, Perth
Chapter 03 · Cognitive Neuroscience

What Does a Hangover Actually Do to Your Mind?

The thesis started with a deceptively simple question: does an alcohol hangover impair the cognition that underlies safe driving? The problem is that mean reaction time, the standard measure in most hangover research, can look normal even when something is wrong. A within-subjects crossover study ran 24 participants through two sessions, one sober and one post-alcohol, with 2,000+ trials of a driving-related decision task per session. Rather than summarising with RT averages, hierarchical Bayesian Drift Diffusion Models were fit to the full RT distributions, recovering drift rate, boundary separation, and non-decision time per participant.

Both drift rate and boundary separation showed hangover-related impairment at a level invisible in the mean RT data. That gap matters: if you're using the wrong measure, you can conclude there's no effect when there plainly is one. Graduated High Distinction on a UWA Global Excellence Scholarship.
