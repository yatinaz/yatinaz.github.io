The Crime Scene

Think of yourself as a detective arriving at a scene. The evidence: 57 mysterious protein machines called reverse transcriptases (RTs). Some of them work for prime editing — a revolutionary way to rewrite DNA. Most do not.

Your job: figure out which ones work, and rank the working ones from best to worst. The catch? You cannot run any experiments. You only have each protein's amino acid sequence — its molecular blueprint.

This is what retroviral_wall_v14.py does. It reads in protein sequences, extracts clever signals from them, and uses machine learning to predict which RTs are active and how efficient they are.

Why Only 57?

Each RT costs hundreds of dollars to test in a wet lab. This is not a big-data problem. It is a small-data problem that requires clever ML — every trick counts when you have fewer samples than most people have browser tabs open.

💡
Small data demands simplicity

With 57 samples, complex models memorize rather than learn. This is why the winning classifier is plain logistic regression — the simplest possible linear model. When data is scarce, fewer parameters beat more parameters almost every time.

Module 3

Speaking the Language of Proteins

Raw protein sequences are gibberish to a model. Feature engineering translates them into structured signals — like filling out a detailed medical intake form.

The Medical Intake Form

When you visit a doctor, you don't hand them your genome. You fill out a form: height, weight, blood pressure, allergies, family history. These structured signals help the doctor quickly assess your health.

Feature engineering does the same for proteins. The raw sequence MKLVFFA... means nothing to a model. But "structural similarity to MMLV = 0.98" and "thumb identity = 66%" are actionable signals.

This pipeline extracts 110+ features in five categories, each telling the model something different about each protein.

The Five Feature Categories

FoldSeek TM-scores

How similar is this RT's 3D shape to known working RTs? The strongest signal. foldseek_TM_MMLV alone gives AUC ~0.78.

Biophysical

Stability, solubility, charge. Does this protein fold properly? Is it stable enough to function? camsol_score, instability_index.

Thermal Stability

Predicted fraction of structure surviving at 55/60/65 degrees C. Heat-resistant enzymes tend to be more active.

New Structural

thumb_fident (rho=0.901) and n_hairpins_found (rho=0.816) — the two strongest regressor signals, absent from the baseline.

ESM-2 PCA

5 components from a 1280-dim protein language model embedding. Captures evolutionary patterns invisible to hand-crafted features. PCA compresses 1280 dims to 5.

The Star Feature: Thumb Subdomain Identity

The thumb subdomain is a structural domain of the RT enzyme — think of it as the "grip" that holds the DNA/RNA hybrid while writing. The thumb_fident feature measures how similar each RT's thumb is to the known best RT (MMLV).

With a Spearman correlation of 0.901, this single feature almost perfectly predicts efficiency among active RTs. It suggests that thumb geometry is the dominant factor in prime editing success — a testable biological hypothesis discovered through ML.

💡
Domain knowledge beats raw features

The generic ESM-2 embeddings (1280 dimensions) are less informative than one carefully chosen structural feature. In ML, knowing what to measure often matters more than measuring everything.

💡
Two models, two imputation strategies

Tree models (XGBoost, CatBoost) get -999 sentinels because they can learn "missing = informative." The LR classifier gets median imputation + z-scaling because linear models need numerically meaningful inputs. The same data, prepared two different ways.

Module 4

The Two-Headed Oracle

One head classifies. One head ranks. A judge blends their testimony. This is the heart of the architecture.

Two Expert Witnesses

Imagine a courtroom. Two expert witnesses testify about each RT protein:

Witness 1 (LR Classifier): "In my professional opinion, this protein has an 82% chance of being active for prime editing." This witness speaks in probabilities — confident yes/no judgments.

Witness 2 (XGB+CB Regressor): "Based on my analysis, this protein would achieve approximately 23.4% editing efficiency." This witness speaks in continuous values — how good, not just whether.

The judge (rank blender) must combine these two different scales of testimony into a single verdict.

Why Ranks Fix Everything

Here is the key insight: a 0.7 probability from logistic regression and a 0.7% efficiency from XGBoost are completely different numbers on completely different scales.

You cannot average them. Instead, rank blending converts each to "what percentile is this sample at?" Now both are on a 0-to-1 scale, and you can blend them.

The formula: final = 0.66 x rank(LR) + 0.34 x rank(regressor). The beta of 0.66 means the classifier gets twice the weight of the regressor.

Module 5

The Cheating Prevention System

The difference between an open-book exam and a closed-book exam. And the subtle bug that inflated scores by 0.10.

The Open-Book Exam Problem

Imagine taking a test where you accidentally had the answer key visible. You would score artificially high — not because you learned the material, but because the answers leaked into your preparation.

Data leakage in ML is exactly this. The model "sees" test data during training, inflating its apparent performance. When deployed on truly new data, it fails.

This pipeline has two anti-cheating systems: LOFO cross-validation and per-fold PCA fitting.

Why LOFO, Not Random K-Fold?

The 57 RTs come from 7 evolutionary families: Retroviral, Retron, LTR Retrotransposon, Group II Intron, CRISPR-associated, Unclassified, and Other.

Random cross-validation would mix family members across train and test. The model could learn "if it looks like an MMLV-family protein, it's active" — memorizing family membership rather than learning biology.

LOFO holds out an entire family per fold. If the Retroviral family (12 RTs, 10 active) is in the test set, the model must predict activity without ever seeing a Retroviral RT in training. This is the real deployment scenario: predicting on an RT from a brand-new evolutionary lineage.

The PCA Leakage Bug

This was the single most impactful fix in the entire project: +0.099 CLS, from 0.677 to 0.776.

The bug: ESM-2 embeddings (1280 dimensions per protein) were reduced to 5 PCA components. But PCA was fitted on all 57 samples before the LOFO split. This meant test-set variance structure leaked into the training features.

❌ Leaky PCA (Before Fix)

1. Load all 57 embeddings
2. Fit PCA on ALL 57 ← LEAK!
3. Transform all 57 to 5 dims
4. Split into train/test folds
5. Train model on "clean" data?

Test data shaped the PCA axes. The model "saw" test patterns encoded in the training features. Score: inflated by +0.10.

✅ Fixed PCA (Per-Fold)

1. Load all 57 embeddings
2. Split into train/test folds
3. Fit PCA on TRAIN ONLY
4. Transform train with PCA
5. Transform test with same PCA

Test data never influences PCA axes. The model is evaluated honestly. Score: real performance.

Code: PCA Inside Each Fold

for fam in sorted(set(families)): tr = np.where(families != fam)[0] te = np.where(families == fam)[0] # PCA fitted inside fold to prevent leakage pf = PCA(n_components=PCA_N, random_state=SEED) pt = pf.fit_transform(all_embs[tr]) pe = pf.transform(all_embs[te])
Loop over each RT family (7 total) Training set = every RT NOT in this family Test set = every RT IN this family Critical: PCA is created fresh each fold New PCA object — never seen any data yet fit_transform: learn PCA axes FROM TRAIN ONLY, then transform train transform: apply the SAME axes to test — but test didn't shape them
💡
Any transformation that "looks at" the data must go inside the CV loop

PCA, StandardScaler, imputation — anything that computes statistics from data (means, variances, principal components) must be fitted only on training data. This applies to every ML project, not just this one. If you see fit_transform before the train/test split, that is a red flag.

Module 6

Reading the Score Card

A report card that penalizes C grades even if you have straight A's elsewhere. Understanding the CLS metric and the journey to 0.803.

What Is CLS?

The CLS metric = harmonic mean of PR-AUC and Weighted Spearman.

Think of a report card. Your regular GPA takes the average — a straight-A student with one C still has a high GPA. But the harmonic mean is harsh: one C drags the whole score down dramatically.

CLS = 2 x PR-AUC x WSpearman / (PR-AUC + WSpearman)

If classification is perfect (PR-AUC = 1.0) but ranking is random (WSpearman = 0.0), CLS = 0. You need both to be good.

Code: Computing CLS

def weighted_spearman(pred, true_eff, weights): pr = np.argsort(np.argsort(pred)).astype(float) tr = np.argsort(np.argsort(true_eff)).astype(float) w = weights / weights.sum() mp = np.dot(w, pr) mt = np.dot(w, tr) cov = np.sum(w * (pr - mp) * (tr - mt)) sp = np.sqrt(np.sum(w * (pr - mp) ** 2)) st = np.sqrt(np.sum(w * (tr - mt) ** 2)) return max(cov / (sp * st), 0.0)
Compute how well predicted ranks match true efficiency ranks Convert predictions to ranks (double argsort trick) Convert true efficiencies to ranks the same way Normalize weights so they sum to 1 (high-efficiency RTs weigh more) Weighted mean of predicted ranks Weighted mean of true ranks Weighted covariance between predicted and true ranks Weighted std dev of predicted ranks Weighted std dev of true ranks Return correlation (cov / product of std devs), floored at 0

Bug fixes beat feature engineering

The PCA leakage fix (v9 to v10) gained +0.099 CLS. All subsequent feature engineering from v10 to v14 gained only +0.027 combined. Before adding complexity, audit your validation pipeline. The biggest gains often come from fixing what you already have.

Era 1 vs Era 2

Era 1 (v1-v7): Optimized for Macro-F1 and Retroviral true positives. Pure classification. The problem: a binary classifier gives identical scores to all active RTs (Weighted Spearman = 0). Great at finding actives, terrible at ranking them.

Era 2 (v8-v14): The CLS metric forced a complete redesign. Now the model needs to both classify AND rank. This is why the two-head architecture exists — one head per sub-metric. The ensemble approach lets each head specialize.
